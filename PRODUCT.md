# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

SleepyWear serves Arabic-speaking Egyptian shoppers, primarily women buying lingerie, pajamas, homewear, satin pieces, curvy sizes, bridal/VIP items, and seasonal collections. Customers are likely browsing on mobile, comparing styles quickly, and looking for reassuring signals around price, quality, delivery, exchange, and trust before checkout.

Store admins use the management surfaces to maintain products, categories, variants, stock, images, orders, and shipping setup. Admin design should remain practical and quiet, but the default public-facing product register is brand-led commerce.

## Product Purpose

SleepyWear is a custom Arabic RTL ecommerce website for women’s lingerie and homewear. It replaces dependence on generic commerce platforms with a controlled, scalable store connected to real product data, local media, checkout, and future operational tooling.

Success means shoppers can quickly understand the catalog, trust the brand, find relevant categories, choose real variants, and place orders with confidence. The storefront should communicate feminine softness, affordability, factory-direct value, and reliable Egyptian delivery without feeling cheap or cluttered.

## Brand Personality

Feminine, clean, soft, commercial, trustworthy, and approachable.

The brand should feel warm and confident rather than luxury-cold. It should suggest factory-direct affordable prices, practical comfort, and everyday beauty for an Egyptian audience.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing-page style.
- Harsh black-heavy UI.
- Random gradients without a clear brand purpose.
- Cluttered marketplace pages that feel noisy or reseller-like.
- Overly sterile luxury styling that hides affordability.
- Backend or data-model changes made just to satisfy visual polish.

## Design Principles

1. Arabic RTL first: layout, copy, scanning order, forms, navigation, and product browsing should feel native for Arabic shoppers.
2. Trust before flourish: product imagery, clear prices, stock/variant clarity, delivery/exchange signals, and checkout reassurance matter more than decorative effects.
3. Soft commercial energy: use the pink and blue brand colors to create a feminine, friendly storefront without turning every section into a loud campaign block.
4. Factory-direct clarity: make affordability and value feel credible, not discount-bin or chaotic.
5. Keep operations practical: admin and management surfaces should prioritize speed, legibility, and predictable controls over storefront decoration.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Aim for WCAG AA contrast and interaction basics wherever practical. Keep text readable in Cairo across Arabic and English, preserve keyboard focus visibility, avoid motion-dependent meaning, and support reduced-motion preferences. Design must handle long Arabic product/category names, mixed Arabic/English labels, mobile browsing, and touch targets suitable for phone-first shoppers.
